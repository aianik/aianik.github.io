Does Transparency About Training Data Change How Users Trust AI?

Studying how transparency about an AI's training data affects how users perceive and trust the system.

Overview

Explainable AI has many ways to show how a model behaves. Knowing how a model behaves helps users follow its logic, catch errors, and decide when to rely on it. But without knowing what the model learned from, that picture is incomplete. This project explores what shifts when training data becomes part of the explanation.

I designed data-centric explanations: descriptions of what an AI model was trained on, written for everyday users rather than technical audiences. Across two sequential user studies, I examined how this form of transparency shapes users' trust and fairness perceptions of AI systems.

My Role

I led this project end to end: conceptualizing the data-centric explanation framework, designing and iterating on the prototype across both studies, recruiting and running all 44 participants, and conducting the full data analysis.

The Problem

AI explainability has largely focused on model behavior: how a model arrives at its outputs. The data a model was trained on has received far less attention, even though it shapes everything: what the model knows, what it misses, and where it might fail. Without visibility into training data, users are left to trust an AI system without the information they need to evaluate whether that trust is warranted. This project set out to understand what changes when that information is made available.

This Work

Research Question

How does transparency about an AI system's training data affect how users perceive and trust the system?

Methods

The project used two sequential studies with different goals and methods. The first was a formative study aimed at understanding how users respond to training data information and what they find meaningful — analyzed through thematic analysis of semi-structured interviews. The second was an evaluative study that tested a refined prototype under controlled conditions, combining post-task questionnaires and post-study interviews to capture both quantitative and qualitative responses.

Identifying the Gap

A review of XAI literature revealed a consistent blind spot: existing explanation approaches addressed model behavior and outputs, but none surfaced information about what the model was trained on. Yet training data is where a model's assumptions, coverage, and potential biases originate. This gap motivated the core question of the project.

Concept Development

Before running any study, I developed the data-centric explanation framework — a structured set of information categories that could describe a training dataset to a non-technical user. This included attributes like data recency, geographic and demographic coverage, collection method, and known limitations. Early sketches explored how to present these categories in a way that was readable without being overwhelming.

From Study 1 to Study 2

Study 1 was exploratory. Study 2 was evaluative. Between them, the design changed substantially based on what participants showed me.

What Study 1 revealed
  • Users wanted control over how deeply they explored each category — fixed sequential presentation felt constraining
  • Collection method and data recency were the most salient attributes; demographic coverage came third
  • Several information categories were confusing or redundant — participants struggled to distinguish between them
  • The reading level of some explanations was too technical for non-expert users
What changed in the redesign
  • Replaced fixed-sequence presentation with a Q&A interface, giving users agency over exploration depth
  • Consolidated and clarified the five information categories based on user confusion patterns
  • Added example questions within each category to prompt engagement and scaffold exploration
  • Simplified language throughout; removed jargon identified in think-aloud sessions

Key Insights

01

Trust is directional, not uniform

Explanations revealing well-curated, representative training data increased confidence. Those surfacing gaps or imbalances reduced it. Users proved to be motivated, capable auditors when given the right information.

02

Data provenance unlocks fairness reasoning

Without training data context, most users accepted AI outputs at face value. With it, they actively questioned demographic representation, identified potential biases, and sought justification for model decisions.

03

Three data attributes drove most reactions

Data recency, geographic and demographic coverage, and collection method were the attributes users consistently anchored their trust assessments on, more than statistical properties or sample size.

04

Context shapes interpretation

Data-centric explanations had the greatest impact in high-stakes domains like hiring and criminal justice, where users already held strong prior beliefs about fairness and were primed to scrutinize AI behavior.

Design Implications

Core Insight

Transparency is not just about explaining decisions. It is about giving people the context they need to decide how much to trust those decisions in the first place.

Impact This work established training data transparency as a recognized direction in XAI research and directly motivated the three follow-on studies in this portfolio. It was published at ACM CHI 2021.
  1. Make training data a first-class transparency artifact. XAI systems should surface human-readable descriptions of training data alongside output explanations, not as a technical supplement, but as a primary communication channel for building calibrated trust.
  2. Lead with what users actually care about. Provenance attributes like recency, demographic coverage, and collection method are far more influential than abstract statistical properties. Explanation design should prioritize these dimensions.
  3. Design for calibrated trust, not maximum trust. An explanation that lowers trust by revealing a model's real limitations is not a failed transparency design: it is the goal. Honest transparency should be the standard, not polished reassurance.
