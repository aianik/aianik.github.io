How Much Detail Should a Training Data Explanation Include?

A mixed factorial study on information depth and progressive disclosure in training dataset explanations, asking whether more detail helps or overwhelms users trying to critique an AI system.

Overview

Every explanation designer faces the same dilemma: give users enough information to reason critically, and you risk overwhelming them. Keep it simple, and you leave them without the tools to scrutinize what matters.

This project tackles that tension directly. Using a mixed 2×2 factorial design, I examined how information depth (summary vs. detailed) and progressive disclosure (present vs. absent) interact to shape user perceptions, cognitive load, and the quality of critiques users produce about AI systems. The findings complicate the standard advice to simplify: participants consistently preferred detailed explanations and produced more accurate critiques with them, even though they required more cognitive effort.

My Role

I designed and prototyped all four explanation conditions for the 2×2 study, each internally consistent while differing only on the intended variables. I piloted the materials with a separate group to confirm that each condition was experienced as intended before the main study, recruited and ran all 32 participants remotely over Zoom, coded critique accuracy and topic coverage alongside a second researcher, and conducted the statistical analysis including Wilcoxon Signed-Rank tests, Mann-Whitney U tests, repeated measures ANOVA, and Pearson chi-squared tests. I also conducted and analyzed the semi-structured interviews using directed qualitative content analysis.

The Problem

Rich, detailed explanations provide the information needed for informed evaluation. But they also impose cognitive demands that can impede the very reasoning they aim to support. Sparse summaries are easier to process but consistently leave users without the context needed to think critically or identify problems.

The Tradeoff

More information means better reasoning potential, but higher cognitive cost. The question is not which side to pick: it is whether a disclosure strategy can let users have both. This study shows the answer is more nuanced than either camp expects.

Research Questions

RQ 1

How does information depth (summary vs. detailed) affect user perceptions of the AI system and the quality of their critiques?

RQ 2

Does progressive disclosure reduce cognitive load associated with detailed explanations without sacrificing critique quality?

RQ 3

Are there interaction effects between information depth and progressive disclosure on user perceptions and critique outcomes?

The Gap

Prior work on training dataset explanations showed users value the information they provide. But the question of how much detail to include remained open. Training dataset explanations are substantially longer than most XAI artifacts, and established findings on information depth from shorter explanations did not clearly generalize to them.

Designing the Study

I crossed two variables: information depth (summary vs. detailed) and progressive disclosure (present vs. absent). I chose depth as a within-subjects factor so participants could directly compare both levels. Progressive disclosure was between-subjects to keep session length manageable; sessions averaged two hours. I prototyped all four explanation interfaces before a pilot study confirmed each condition was experienced as intended.

Study
Information Depth and Progressive Disclosure in Training Dataset Explanations
Mixed 2×2 Design n = 32 Critique Task NASA-TLX Semi-Structured Interviews

Each participant critiqued two AI systems (an automated hiring system and an automated admission system), one using a summary explanation and one using a detailed explanation, in counterbalanced order. Both systems were drawn from prior work on training dataset explanations. Participants used Userback to annotate interface elements and enter critique comments. After each critique, they completed Likert-scale questionnaires on trust, fairness, perceived understanding, perceived learning, and cognitive load via the NASA-TLX, plus five comprehension questions. A semi-structured interview closed each session.

Key Findings

Detailed explanations improved every perception measure. Participants reported significantly higher perceived understanding (p<.001), trust (p=.002), fairness (p<.001), and learning (p<.001) with the detailed explanation, but also significantly higher cognitive load (p=.004).

Detailed explanations produced more and more accurate critiques. Participants provided more comments with detailed explanations (mean = 9.84 vs. 7.16, p<.001) and more accurate ones (241/315 accurate vs. 153/229 with summary). Only 1 detailed comment was coded as completely inaccurate, versus 6 in summary.

Progressive disclosure affected perceived learning, not cognitive load. Participants with progressive disclosure reported significantly higher perceived learning (p=.012), particularly with the detailed explanation (p=.017). Cognitive load did not differ significantly between disclosure conditions (p=.543).

Participants unequivocally preferred detailed explanations. Interview data showed a clear preference for detailed explanations despite the higher cognitive effort, with participants stating that the summary left them without enough context to trust or critique the system.

The 2×2 Design

Information depth was a within-subjects factor: every participant used both summary and detailed explanations, on different scenarios in counterbalanced order. Progressive disclosure was a between-subjects factor: half the participants saw all information upfront, the other half used an interactive interface that revealed one category at a time and required clicking to expand individual questions.

Methods

🧪

Mixed 2×2 Factorial Design

Information depth (summary vs. detailed) as within-subjects factor; progressive disclosure (present vs. absent) as between-subjects factor. 32 participants, 2-hour sessions, conducted remotely over Zoom.

🧠

NASA-TLX

Validated cognitive load measure capturing mental demand, effort, and frustration. Applied after each critique scenario to compare load across both depth conditions.

📝

Critique Task + Coding

Participants critiqued two AI systems using a simplified SWOT format. Two researchers coded all 544 comments for topic coverage and accuracy using a scheme from prior work.

💬

Semi-Structured Interviews

Post-study interviews probed preferences, interpretation strategies, and reactions to the two explanation levels. Analyzed via directed qualitative content analysis.

Key Insights

01

Users prefer detail even when it costs them

Participants unanimously preferred detailed explanations despite reporting significantly higher cognitive load. They tolerated extra effort when it meant understanding the system more fully. The cost of confusion was worse than the cost of effort.

02

Depth improves accuracy but narrows focus

Detailed explanations produced more accurate critiques and more comments, but participants disproportionately focused on the data collection category. Summary explanations led to shorter critiques with more balanced coverage across topics.

03

Progressive disclosure shapes the experience, not the load

Progressive disclosure did not reduce cognitive load, but it significantly increased participants' sense of learning, especially with the detailed explanation. Its value is experiential, not computational: it makes engagement feel more intentional and exploratory.

04

Ease of use does not mean adequacy

Summary explanations were easier to process, but that ease did not translate into better critiques or stronger understanding of the AI system. Subjective comfort and actual reasoning quality pointed in different directions.

Design Implications

Core Insight

Detailed explanations consistently outperformed summaries on every perception measure and on critique accuracy, even at higher cognitive cost. For training dataset explanations, which are longer than most XAI artifacts and used during onboarding when users are forming an initial mental model, the evidence favors providing comprehensive information over minimizing it. Data-centric explanations earn this preference precisely because users want to understand what the data says about the system's limitations, not just that the system exists.

  1. Default to detail for onboarding and evaluation tasks. When users are forming an initial mental model of an AI system or are tasked with evaluating it, detailed explanations produce better outcomes. Summaries should not be treated as a safer or more user-friendly default.
  2. Use progressive disclosure to improve the learning experience, not to manage cognitive load. Progressive disclosure did not reduce cognitive load in this study, but it did increase perceived learning for detailed explanations. Its value is in making exploration feel intentional, not in hiding information to lighten the load.
  3. Consider interface mechanisms that broaden topic engagement. Detailed explanations led participants to focus heavily on data collection, the first category in the interface. Designers should consider how to draw attention to other types of information, through visual emphasis, prompting, or ordering, when comprehensive coverage is the goal.
  4. Do not optimize explanations for comfort. Users rated summary explanations as easier but did not produce better critiques with them. Explanation design should be evaluated against reasoning and accuracy measures, not just satisfaction scores.

This study provides empirical evidence on how information depth shapes users' comprehension and critiques of AI systems, and offers preliminary findings on how progressive disclosure shapes user experience in explanation onboarding. The finding that users prefer and perform better with detailed explanations, despite higher cognitive load, directly challenges the common recommendation to simplify AI explanations.
